ElectroFind — Charge & Cost Calculations

This document describes how ElectroFind estimates charging time and cost for a Hyundai Kona Electric (Long Range, 77.4 kWh usable battery).

1. The Charge Curve

The car's Battery Management System (BMS) limits how much power it will accept at each state of charge (SoC). ElectroFind encodes this as 101 data points extracted from a measured charge curve, one per integer SoC percentage (0–100%). Each point is a (SoC%, kW) pair representing the maximum power the car will accept at that SoC.

Key shape

SoC rangeAccepted powerNotes
0–10%50 → 91 kWRamp-up from cold
11–61%~92–100 kWFull-power plateau
62–67%~75 kWFirst taper
68–73%~45 kWSecond taper
74–80%~37–46 kWGradual reduction
81–82%~25–31 kWSharp drop

93–100%24 → 8 kWFinal taper to full

Between integer SoC values, power is computed by linear interpolation:

powerAtSoc(soc) = curve[floor(soc)] + (curve[ceil(soc)] − curve[floor(soc)]) × (soc − floor(soc))

2. Charging Efficiency

Not all power drawn from the charger reaches the battery. The loss depends on whether the charger is AC or DC:

Charger typeThresholdEfficiencyReason
DC fast charge ≥ 22 kW 95% The charger converts AC→DC externally. Losses are small and occur outside the car.
AC charge < 22 kW 88% The car's onboard charger converts AC→DC internally, generating heat (~12% lost).
Conservative estimate: costs and times are calculated using the grid draw (what the meter measures), not the energy that reaches the battery. On AC charging you pay for roughly 12% more kWh than go into the battery.

3. Time Simulation

The simulation steps through the charge in 0.1% SoC increments from startSoc to targetSoc (or until the stay time is exhausted).

Per-step calculation

  1. Energy per step — fixed, regardless of charger speed:
    energyPerStep = 77.4 kWh × 0.001 = 0.0774 kWh
  2. Effective power — the lower of what the charger can deliver and what the car will accept:
    effectiveKw = min(chargerMaxKw, powerAtSoc(currentSoc))
  3. Time for this step — divided by efficiency so AC sessions correctly take longer:
    timeStep (minutes) = energyPerStep ÷ (effectiveKw × efficiency) × 60

    Operator precedence: (energyPerStep ÷ (effectiveKw × efficiency)) gives hours; × 60 converts to minutes.

  4. Stay-time check — if a stay limit is set and the next step would exceed it, a partial step is applied proportional to the remaining time, then the simulation stops:
    remaining = stayMinutes − accumulatedMinutes
    fraction = remaining ÷ timeStep
    energyAdded = energyPerStep × fraction
    socGained = 0.1% × fraction

Outputs

FieldMeaning
endSocPercentSoC reached at the end of the session
energyKwhNet energy delivered to the battery (kWh)
billedEnergyKwhGrid draw = energyKwh ÷ efficiency — what the meter records
chargeMinutesTotal time actively charging
reachedTargetWhether the target SoC was reached before the stay limit

4. Two Simulation Modes

ModeHow it runsWhat it answers
Optimal No stay limit (stayMinutes = null). Runs to target SoC. "How long will it take to reach my target, and what will it cost?"
Stay Stay limit set. Stops at whichever comes first: target SoC or time limit. "If I stay for N minutes, what SoC will I reach and what will it cost?"

5. Cost Calculation

Given a SimResult, the total cost is:

cost = billedEnergyKwh × pricePerKwh
+ connectionFee
+ chargingRatePerMin × chargeMinutes
+ parkingRatePerMin × idleMinutes

Where:

idleMinutes = max(0, stayMinutes − chargeMinutes)

idleMinutes is the time after the car has finished charging but the session is still running (e.g. the car reached 80% after 25 minutes but you stayed for 30). Chargers that levy an idle/parking fee apply it to this window.

Not all chargers publish all components. Where a field is absent it is treated as zero. Costs should be treated as estimates — actual billing may vary by network and tariff.

6. Limitations

  • The charge curve is specific to the Hyundai Kona Electric Long Range (77.4 kWh). Other vehicles will charge differently.
  • The curve was measured under nominal conditions. Cold batteries charge more slowly; very hot batteries may also throttle.
  • Efficiency figures (95% DC / 88% AC) are typical values. Actual efficiency varies by charger, cable, and temperature.
  • The simulation does not model pre-conditioning, cell balancing pauses, or network-imposed power limits.
  • Tariff data comes from Electroverse and may be incomplete or out of date.
